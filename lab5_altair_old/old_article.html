An Analysis of Election Polls

The 2020 election cycle is shaping up to be one of the most exciting elections in recent history. Current President Donald Trump is a polarizing figure, but so are some of the candidates running for the Democratic nominee. In this article, we will examine three main findings from a FiveThirtyEight dataset that has voter favorability poll results for the 2020 general presidential election. The question presented to respondents was: "If Candidate X were the Democratic nominee, who would you vote for?" (Based on the assumption that President Trump will remain the Republican nominee.)

1. Do general election favorability ratings tend to follow primary elections?

The primary election cycle has already started in a few states (e.g. Iowa, New Hampshire, and Nevada). We know the results of these primary elections is not an exact comparison to the general presidential election, since this data matches Democratic candidates against Trump, but it may inform us about whether or not support for a particular candidate might carry into the general election. For instance, we can look at people's polling preferences in Iowa, New Hampshire and Nevada, compared to the known outcomes of the primaries/caucuses. We know that Iowa is still being disputed (but Buttigieg and Sanders are functionally tied for 1st place), Sanders won New Hampshire, but Pete Buttigieg (2nd place) was very close to his numbers. In Nevada, Bernie won by a much bigger percentage. None of the candidates do very well in head-to-head matchups against Trump in Iowa. In Nevada and New Hampshire, Sanders beats Trump in about 80% of the polls. However, we see that in both New Hampshire and Nevada, Bernie Sanders was the candidate that had the highest % of polls that had him beating Trump in the general election.

2. Do candidates' favorability change by polling method?

It might also be useful to look at candidate performance by polling methodology. We know that Bernie Sanders does extremely well among young voters, who might be more likely to answer polls conducted online or via text. Biden, on the other hand, performs well among older voters, who may be more likely to answer polls conducted over the phone. The chart below shows the breakdown of candidates' favorability percentages by region and polling methodology.

From the data, we see that for a given region, the Democratic candidates all seem to have similar trends by polling methods. For instance, in the Midwest, Sanders, Warren, and Biden all do the worst in Automated Phone polls. In the South, however, all of the candidates do the worst in Live Phone/Online polls. This seems to indicate that regional differences may be a stronger factor in how a candidate performs in polls, rather than polling methodology.
